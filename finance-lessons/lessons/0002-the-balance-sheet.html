Lesson 2 · Foundations

The Balance Sheet: assets, liabilities, and your real scoreboard

Continues Lesson 1 · ~8 minutes · grounded in Audit §1–2

Lesson 1 ended with a claim: net worth is the scoreboard; cash flow is the engine. This lesson opens the scoreboard. It is the single thing Hoalu most badly lacks.

Definition

Asset
Something you own that holds value — cash, savings, a paid-off car, a home, investments. Measured at a point in time (a stock).
Liability
Something you owe — a credit-card balance, a car loan, a mortgage, unpaid taxes. Also a stock.
Net worth
Assets − Liabilities, at a single date. The bottom line of the balance sheet.

A household balance sheet, in one table

Pick any date — say, tonight. List what you own and what you owe. The rest is arithmetic.

Net worth = 450,000,000 − 1,295,000,000 = −845,000,000 ₫

Negative net worth is normal early. A young household with a fresh mortgage almost always owes more than it owns. The goal is not to start positive — it is to make the number rise over years. Track the trend, not the snapshot.

Why net worth beats income as the scoreboard

A high earner with high debt and zero savings has a large flow (income) and a shrinking stock (net worth). A modest earner who saves consistently has a smaller flow but a rising stock. The Millionaire Next Door made this famous: the people who actually accumulate wealth are rarely the highest earners — they are the ones whose net worth climbs every year because they spend less than they earn and invest the gap.

Income matters only insofar as it feeds the balance sheet. If your income rises but your net worth does not, the income is leaking out somewhere. The balance sheet is the lie detector.

In Hoalu — the scoreboard is blank

Hoalu cannot produce the table above. Concretely:

  • No balances on wallets — there is no balance column, so "Cash & checking" and "Savings" cannot be listed. (Audit §1)
  • No liabilities modelled — a credit card is just a wallet name; a car loan or mortgage has no home at all. (Audit §2)
  • No net-worth series — no chart of net worth over time, the one trend that actually matters.

The app tracks the engine (income/expense) but cannot show the scoreboard it is supposed to be driving. That is the headline gap this course will keep returning to.

The win

Tonight, write your own balance sheet on paper: every account you own and every debt you owe, with today's date at the top. Whatever the number — positive, zero, negative — you now have a baseline. From here, the only question is whether it rises. That is what a finance app must help you see, and what Hoalu must learn to show.

Primary source to read next

The Millionaire Next Door — Stanley & Danko. The book's central finding: net worth, not income, is the true measure of financial position, and wealth accumulators are distinguished by behaviour, not paycheque size. It is the clearest case for why Hoalu must surface net worth above all else.

Ask your teacher. "Should my car count as an asset if it loses value every year?" "Is a mortgage 'good debt'?" "How often should I rebuild the balance sheet?" — ask. The answers steer the next lesson.
