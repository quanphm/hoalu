Lesson 12 · The long game

Investing basics: why cash loses, and how net worth grows

Continues Lesson 11 · ~9 minutes · grounded in Audit, missing features

Saving preserves money. Investing grows it. The distinction is not optional: over decades, cash loses to inflation; invested capital compounds. This lesson is why the balance sheet (Lesson 2) needs an investment line — and why Hoalu, which has no investment entity, cannot show the engine that actually builds wealth.

The three forces

Definition

Inflation
The slow rise in prices that erodes the purchasing power of idle cash. A bank balance earning nothing loses real value every year.
Compounding
Returns earned on prior returns. The longer money is invested, the steeper the curve — most of the growth happens in the later years.
Risk premium
The extra return markets pay for taking on risk (stocks over bonds over cash). Higher expected return is the reward for accepting volatility.

The household question is not whether to invest but how much of the balance sheet belongs in growth assets versus cash. Cash covers needs and near-term goals (Lessons 3, 10); investments cover the long game — money you will not touch for years.

The simplest portfolio most people need

Source. J.L. Collins's The Simple Path to Wealth and the Bogleheads wiki both land on the same answer for non-professionals: a small number of low-cost, broad-market index funds, held for decades. No stock-picking, no market timing.

A broad-market index fund holds a tiny slice of every company in a market, so you own the whole market's average return at near-zero cost. The classic three-fund portfolio is: a domestic stock index, an international stock index, and a bond index. The split between stocks and bonds sets your risk: more stocks = more growth and more volatility; more bonds = calmer but slower. Age and time horizon decide the mix.

The one graph that changes behaviour

Compounding is a curve, not a line. Invest 1,000,000 ₫/month at a 7% real return:

  • After 10 years: ~170,000,000 ₫ (you put in 120,000,000)
  • After 20 years: ~520,000,000 ₫ (you put in 240,000,000)
  • After 30 years: ~1,200,000,000 ₫ (you put in 360,000,000)

The contributions tripled; the result grew sevenfold from year 10 to year 30. The last years did most of the work. The lesson: start now, not later — a decade of delay costs more than it seems, because the missed years are the ones that would have compounded hardest.

In Hoalu — the growth engine is absent

  • There is no investment account entity, no holdings, no tickers, no portfolio valuation. "Investments" is only an income-category name. (Audit, missing features)
  • The balance sheet (Lesson 2) therefore cannot show the line that actually grows net worth over decades — only static cash and depreciating assets.
  • Without an investment line, the app cannot chart net-worth-over-time in a way that means anything, because the curve that should dominate the chart is missing.

The smallest useful version: an investment account with manual holdings and a current value (market value as a stock), feeding net worth. Automatic price feeds and rebalancing come later — the user first needs the line to exist at all.

The win

If you have no investments: open a brokerage, buy one broad-market index fund, set a small monthly auto-invest. The amount matters less than starting — the curve in this lesson rewards time above all. If you already invest: list your holdings and their rough allocation (stocks vs bonds vs cash). That allocation is your real risk dial; the app should eventually hold it.
