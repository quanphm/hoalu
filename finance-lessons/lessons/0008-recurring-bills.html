Lesson 8 · Operations

Recurring bills & subscriptions done right

Continues Lesson 7 · ~8 minutes · grounded in Audit §9–11

Recurring bills are the part of Hoalu that already works well — occurrence projection, the overdue/today/upcoming view, the "log a payment" link to expenses. This lesson is partly a celebration, partly the edge cases the engine gets wrong, and partly the household habit the feature should enable: the subscription audit.

The model, done well

A recurring bill is a definition — an amount, a currency, a frequency, an anchor date, and a destination wallet and category. From that definition, the engine projects occurrences: each expected payment on its due date, initially unpaid. When the user logs a payment, an expense is created and the matching occurrence is marked paid. The bill becomes two layers: a stable template and a stream of actual events. That is exactly the right shape, and Hoalu has it.

Why two layers matters. Without occurrences you only know the bill exists; you never know whether this month's instalment was paid. The occurrence layer is what turns a reminder into a trackable obligation — and what lets the upcoming-bills view show paid/unpaid status per instance.

The household habit: the subscription audit

Recurring bills are where money leaks silently. A household accumulates streaming, software, gym, and app subscriptions that auto-renew forever, each small enough to ignore. The fix is a periodic subscription audit: list every recurring charge, mark each used or not used, and cancel the second kind. The audit pays for itself in an afternoon. An app should make this trivial — one screen, total monthly recurring, sorted by amount, with a "last used?" nudge. Hoalu's recurring-bills list is 80% of this feature already.

Three edge cases the engine mishandles

In Hoalu — where the engine cracks

  • dueDay = 0 is accepted but invalid. The schema allows 0–31. For a monthly bill, day 0 means "last day of the previous month" — occurrences land in the wrong month. The field can't validate because one number serves both weekly (0–6) and monthly (1–31) frequencies. (Audit §9)
  • "One-time" and "custom" recurring bills generate nothing. A "one-time recurring bill" is a contradiction; the generator has no branch for it and returns empty — silently. The schema permits it. (Audit §10)
  • Income's repeat is decorative. income.repeat exists but there is no recurringBillId and no occurrence logic — so a salary marked "monthly" never projects. Recurring logic exists only for expenses. (Audit §11)

The 31st-of-February question

A bill "due on the 31st" has no due date in February, April, June, etc. Two defensible policies: clamp (charge on the 28th/30th, never skip) or skip (no charge that month). Hoalu clamps — which is fine — but should say so to the user, because a bill charged on the 28th that the user expects on the 31st is a reconciliation surprise. The bigger lesson: every edge case in scheduling should be visible to the user, not silently absorbed.

The general principle

Any time a finance engine makes a quiet decision for the user — clamping a date, skipping a month, zeroing a missing amount — that decision must be surfaced. Silent behaviour in a money app is a bug, even when the behaviour is reasonable, because the user cannot reconcile what they do not know happened.

The win

Do a subscription audit tonight. Open Hoalu's recurring-bills list (or write one on paper): every recurring charge, its monthly equivalent, and "used / not used this month." Cancel the nots. Then sum the monthly total of what remains — that single number is your recurring floor, the minimum the household must clear each month before any discretionary spending.
