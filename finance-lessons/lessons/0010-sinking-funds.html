Lesson 10 · Planning

Savings goals & sinking funds: give the future a place to live

Continues Lesson 9 · ~8 minutes · grounded in Audit, missing features

Some expenses are large, known, and months away — annual insurance, a tax bill, a trip home, a new phone. The mistake is to treat them as surprises when they arrive. A sinking fund turns a future lump into many small present contributions. It is budgeting across time, not just across categories.

Definition

Sinking fund
Money set aside gradually for a known future expense of a known amount and date. Each month you sink a little in, so the lump never hits cash flow all at once.
Monthly contribution
Target amount ÷ months remaining. The amount to save each month so the fund hits its target by the due date.
Envelope
An allocation of money to a purpose within a single savings account — the fund exists as a labelled slice, not a separate bank account.

The arithmetic

monthly contribution = target amount ÷ months remaining

Annual insurance of 12,000,000 ₫ due in 12 months → 1,000,000 ₫/month. A trip of 30,000,000 ₫ in 10 months → 3,000,000 ₫/month. The lumpy future becomes a smooth, budgetable present line — exactly like a recurring bill, but where you pay yourself first.

The reconciliation test. A sinking fund changes where money is allocated, not whether you have it. Moving cash into the fund is a transfer (Lesson 6), not an expense — your net worth is unchanged. The expense is recorded later, when the purchase happens, paid from the fund.

Why this is different from "saving"

Generic savings protects against the unknown. A sinking fund is the opposite — it is for a specific future expense you can name and date. The household difference is huge: without sinking funds, known future lumps masquerade as emergencies when they arrive, and the emergency fund gets drained by things that were never emergencies. With sinking funds, the emergency fund stays reserved for the genuinely unexpected.

In Hoalu — the future has no address

  • There is no goal or allocation entity — money in a savings account is an undifferentiated balance, with no label saying "this 6,000,000 is for insurance." (Audit, missing features)
  • Without allocations, the app cannot compute "monthly contribution to keep on track," cannot warn when a fund is behind, and cannot show a due-date countdown.
  • This compounds with the missing transfer (Lesson 6) and missing per-category budget (Lesson 7): sinking funds need both to exist as a first-class concept.

The smallest useful version: a goal with target amount, target date, linked wallet, and a rollup of allocated transfers — then a card per goal showing "on track / behind by X months." That is the feature that turns a savings account from a pile into a plan.

The win

List every known future expense in the next 12 months — insurance, tax, travel, a replacement phone, school fees. For each, write the amount and the month it's due. Divide amount by months remaining. The list of monthly contributions is a new set of "bills you owe yourself" — and it almost always reveals you are under-saving for something specific.
