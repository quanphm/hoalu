Lesson 11 · The balance-sheet half

Debt & loans: amortization, and avalanche vs. snowball

Continues Lesson 10 · ~9 minutes · grounded in Audit, missing features

Lesson 4 covered the worst debt — revolving credit cards. This lesson covers the rest: loans with a principal, an interest rate, and a term. Hoalu has no entity for any of them. Understanding amortization and the two payoff strategies is what turns a scary balance into a plan.

The anatomy of a loan

Definition

Principal
The amount you still owe — the part of the balance that is yours to pay down. A stock.
Interest
The cost of borrowing — a flow the lender charges each period on the outstanding principal.
Term
The number of payments scheduled to clear the loan.
Amortization
The schedule that splits each fixed payment into interest (on the remaining principal) and principal reduction. Early payments are mostly interest; late payments are mostly principal.
The front-loaded trap. Because interest is charged on the outstanding principal, early payments are almost all interest — you barely dent the principal for years. This is why a 30-year mortgage feels stuck for the first decade. Extra payments against principal, early, shorten the loan far more than the same money applied years later.

The minimum payment is a trap

Every loan quotes a minimum payment. It is sized to stretch the loan across the full term — maximising the interest you pay. Paying only the minimum on a credit card is the single most expensive habit a household can have: a 20,000,000 ₫ balance at 25% APR, paying the ~2% minimum, takes decades and costs more in interest than the original purchase. The rule: always pay more than the minimum, against the highest-APR debt first.

Two strategies for multiple debts

AvalancheSnowball
OrderHighest APR firstSmallest balance first
CostMathematically least interestSlightly more interest
MotivationSlower visible winsFast wins as balances hit zero
Best forDisciplined, pure-maths typesPeople who need momentum

Both work the same way: pay minimums on every debt, then throw all spare cash at the target debt until it is gone, then roll that freed-up amount onto the next target. Avalanche is cheapest; snowball is most motivating. The best strategy is the one you will actually stick to — a plan you abandon saves nothing.

In Hoalu — debt is invisible

  • There is no loan entity — no principal, no APR, no term, no amortization schedule, no monthly payment field. (Audit, missing features)
  • A mortgage, a car loan, a student loan — none can be represented. They appear, at best, as expenses (the monthly payment) with no link to the underlying debt.
  • No payoff planner — the app cannot show "avalanche vs. snowball, total interest difference, debt-free date." That single view is what makes a debt plan real.
  • The credit card (Lesson 4) is the most expensive debt most households carry, and it cannot be modelled as debt either.

Debt is the heaviest item on most households' balance sheets. An app that cannot show it cannot help manage it.

The win

List every debt you owe: balance, APR, minimum payment, term. Sort by APR (avalanche). Note how much spare cash you can put above the minimums each month. That spare cash, aimed at the top of the list until it is gone, then rolled onto the next — is a complete debt-elimination plan you can hold in one paragraph.
