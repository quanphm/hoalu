Glossary

The canonical language of this course. Every lesson adheres to these terms; update in place as understanding deepens.

Foundations

Stock — point-in-time quantity
A quantity measured at a single moment. "How much right now?" — a bank balance today, debt as of a date, net worth this instant.
Avoid: amount, total (ambiguous with flow).
Flow — period quantity
A quantity measured over a period. "How much moved during March?" — salary earned this month, groceries spent this month, a savings rate.
Avoid: amount, total (ambiguous with stock).
Balance sheet
The stock statement — assets and liabilities at a single date, yielding net worth. A snapshot, not a range.
Cash-flow statement
The flow statement — income and expenses over a date range, yielding savings. The engine that changes the balance sheet.
Net worth
Assets − liabilities, at a single date. The scoreboard; the true measure of financial position.
Avoid: wealth (vague), balance (means a stock on one account, not the whole sheet).

Balance sheet

Asset
Something you own that holds value — cash, savings, a car, a home, investments. A stock.
Liability
Something you owe — a card balance, a loan, a mortgage, unpaid tax. A stock.
Opening balance
The amount in an account at the moment you start tracking. Without it, no current balance can reconcile.
Reconciliation identity
current balance = opening + Σ income − Σ expenses − Σ transfers out + Σ transfers in. The rule every account must satisfy.
Credit limit
The most a card lender will let you owe at once. A stock, set by the bank.
Balance owed — outstanding balance
What you currently owe on a credit card. A stock that rises with purchases, falls with payments.
Available credit
Credit limit − balance owed. How much more you can spend right now.
Statement balance
What you owed on the statement closing date. Paying this in full by the due date avoids all interest.
Grace period
The window between the statement closing date and the payment due date, during which no interest accrues — if the statement balance is paid in full.
APR — annual percentage rate
The yearly interest rate applied to any card balance carried past the due date.
Utilisation
Balance owed ÷ credit limit. A high ratio hurts creditworthiness; the live constraint to watch.
Transactor
A cardholder who pays the statement balance in full each month — pays no interest, uses the bank's money free.
Revolver
A cardholder who carries a balance past the due date — pays interest, the card is a liability.

Cash flow

Income
Money earned from outside the household over a period — a flow in. Increases net worth.
Expense
Money spent on consumed goods/services over a period — a flow out. Decreases net worth.
Savings — cash flow (positive)
Income − expenses over a period, when positive. "Not spent," wherever it lands — cash, debt paydown, or investments.
Savings rate
Savings ÷ income × 100, over a month or year. The percentage of income kept; the single most predictive number for financial trajectory.
Transfer
Moving money between accounts you own. Changes where money sits, not how much you have — no effect on cash flow or net worth.
Recurring floor
The total monthly cost of all recurring bills and subscriptions. The minimum the household must clear before any discretionary spending.

Planning

Budget
A plan for spending, set before the period begins — a cap or allocation. Distinct from a record (what was spent).
Cap
The maximum planned for a category in a period. The budget number compared against actual spend.
Zero-based budgeting
A system that gives every unit of income a job before spending, so income − allocations = 0. Tightest control.
Envelope — allocation
A labelled slice of money assigned to a purpose within a single account — the fund exists as a label, not a separate bank account.
50/30/20
A proportion rule: ~50% needs, ~30% wants, ~20% savings/debt. The simplest budgeting system; coarsest control.
Rollover
Carrying a category's underspend forward as next month's buffer, or overspend as a debt against next month's cap.
Sinking fund
Money set aside gradually for a known future expense of known amount and date. Monthly contribution = target ÷ months remaining.
Emergency fund
Reserve for unknown bad events, typically 3–6 months of expenses. Distinct from sinking funds (which are for known future expenses).

Debt

Principal
The amount of a loan still owed — the part you pay down. A stock.
Interest
The cost of borrowing, charged each period on the outstanding principal. A flow.
Term
The number of scheduled payments to clear a loan.
Amortization
The schedule splitting each fixed payment into interest and principal reduction. Early payments are mostly interest; late ones mostly principal.
Minimum payment
The lowest a lender will accept. Sized to stretch the loan across the full term, maximising interest paid — a trap.
Avalanche
A debt-payoff strategy targeting the highest-APR debt first. Mathematically the least total interest.
Snowball
A debt-payoff strategy targeting the smallest balance first. Slightly more interest, but faster visible wins for motivation.

Investing

Inflation
The slow rise in prices that erodes the purchasing power of idle cash.
Compounding
Returns earned on prior returns. The longer money is invested, the steeper the curve — most growth is in the later years.
Index fund
A low-cost fund holding a slice of every company in a market, capturing the market's average return. No stock-picking.
Three-fund portfolio
A simple portfolio of domestic stocks, international stocks, and bonds. The split between stocks and bonds sets the risk.
Risk premium
The extra return markets pay for accepting risk (stocks over bonds over cash).

Financial independence

Crossover point
The moment investments can generate enough to cover expenses forever — work becomes optional.
FI number — financial-independence number
The invested net worth needed to hit the crossover. ≈ annual expenses ÷ withdrawal rate (≈ ×25 at 4%).
4% rule
A historically-safe withdrawal rate: withdraw 4% of the starting balance in year one, adjust for inflation after. Inverse of "×25."
Withdrawal rate
The percentage of a portfolio drawn each year in retirement. Lower is safer; 3.5% is often preferred for longer horizons.
Sequence-of-returns risk
The danger that poor market returns early in retirement break a plan that averages fine over the period.

Terms are added as the course teaches them. When understanding deepens or a definition turns out wrong, revise in place — do not leave stale entries.
